TIME TO MARKET

TIME TO MARKET

TIME TO MARKET

[ MVP ]

Achieve your ** Minimum Viable Product ** to test your concept.

TIME TO MARKET

[ MVP ]

The ** perimeter ** of your MVP must be ** reduced ** while allowing you to market your product.

Bet on ** Early Adopters ** and get a maximum of ** feedback **.

Your MVP is deployed and usable in ** production **.

TIME TO MARKET

[ FAIL-FAST ]

** Fail ** fast is ** learn ** fast.

TIME TO MARKET

[ FAIL-FAST ]

** Quickly experience ** the solution (a few weeks), collect ** feedback ** from your users and learn from your mistakes.

Do not be afraid to change everything **.

Do not forget, ** you will fail! **

TIME TO MARKET

[ KISS ]

Keep It Simple and Stupid.

Why make it complicated when it can be simple ?

TIME TO MARKET

[ KISS ]

** Avoid over-engineering **, if a "paper" model or a Google Form is enough to test your concept, do not go further.

Stay simple! Both technically and functionally.

TIME TO MARKET

[ PRODUCTIVITY ]

Specify less, ** expand more **.

TIME TO MARKET

[ PRODUCTIVITY ]

Limit your specifications to the bare essentials, ** focus on the "what" ** rather than the "how".

The product must be the most ** self-documented ** possible.

The documentation must be versioned in the same way as the code.

TIME TO MARKET

[ CORE BUSINESS ]

The ** core business ** should not be a hindrance to the construction of new services and applications.

TIME TO MARKET

[ CORE BUSINESS ]

The pace of evolution and delivery of the core business must be ** compatible with the agility ** of the services that consume it.

The core business must ** expose services **.

The core business must adopt a ** Event-Driven ** principle, it reports management actions in the form of events.

TIME TO MARKET

[ CONTINUOUS DEPLOYMENT ]

** Deployment in production ** is a non-event.

TIME TO MARKET

[ CONTINUOUS DEPLOYMENT ]

Take advantage of ** continuous deployment ** to adapt ** ** production ** to ** business ** requirements and not the other way around.

** Deployments ** across environments, up to ** production **, must be ** automated ** and ** frequent **.

USER EXPERIENCE

USER EXPERIENCE

USER EXPERIENCE

[ PERCEPTION ]

The ** experience perceived ** by the user is fundamental.

** ergonomics ** is not negotiable.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ PERCEPTION ]

Do not neglect the work of ** UX designers **, it is fundamental in the development of an application.

Integrate the ** feedback ** of your users, this one is essential.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ PERFORMANCE ]

Use ** powerful interfaces ** for both internal and external uses.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ PERFORMANCE ]

The interfaces are geared towards ** efficiency **.

The ** performance ** of an interface saves time **, increases users' ** satisfaction ** and therefore ** saves their frustration **.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ CONTRIBUTORS ]

Do not forget that your ** associates ** are using modern applications at home in UX.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ CONTRIBUTORS ]

Treat ** all your users as "customers" **: Internet users, managers, operators, developers, etc.

Do not underestimate the ** UX effort ** to implement for internal-use management applications.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ ALL MEASURING ]

Everything that can be measured must be.

** Without measure, everything is only opinion **.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ ALL MEASURING ]

Think of the metrics during the ** development ** of the application. ** logs ** must have a ** business as well as technical ** dimension.

Do not neglect the ** performance metrics **, they are fundamental.

The Feature Team provides ** operation **: it is responsible for making the ** application usable **.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ DEGRADATION ]

** Consider degradation ** rather than service interruption in the event of a failure.

USER EXPERIENCE

[ DEGRADATION ]

In ** failure ** of one of the subsystems, ** a degraded ** version of the service must ** be considered ** in the first place rather than an interruption.

With ** Circuit Breakers **, ** isolate a breakdown ** to ** avoid ** its ** impact ** and ** spread ** over the entire ** system **.

HUMAN

HUMAN

HUMAN

[ FEATURE TEAM ]

The team is organized around a ** product ** or a ** service ** rendered.

HUMAN

[ FEATURE TEAM ]

Teams are ** Feature Teams **, organized around a coherent functional set, and composed of all the ** skills ** necessary for this set.

For example: Business Expert + Web Developer + Java Developer + Architect + DBA + Operational.

The ** responsibility ** is ** collective **, the Feature Team has the power necessary for this responsibility.

HUMAN

[ EVE ]

The organization must be a ** sleep engine **

The day before is part of the job.

HUMAN

[ EVE ]

The organization must be a ** day-care ** engine by setting up systems such as ** continuing education ** or ** business universities **.

Feel free to combine them with other more ** informal ** ways such as: ** Coding Dojos **, ** Brown Bag Lunchs **, ** External ** Conferences.

HUMAN

[ CO-CONSTRUCTION ]

Break the barriers between trades, bet on the ** convergence ** goals.

HUMAN

[ CO-CONSTRUCTION ]

To break the barriers between trades, it is not enough to group people around a common product in a common place.

The ** Agile Approaches ** remove these barriers to ensure ** convergence of objectives **.

These practices are an integral part of the keys to success, the organization is the guarantor.

HUMAN

[ DEVOPS ]

** DevOps ** practices allow walls to fall between Build and Run.

HUMAN

[ DEVOPS ]

Adopt ** DevOps ** to converge ** Dev ** and ** Ops ** towards a common goal: ** serve the organization **.

** The trades remain different **! DevOps does not mean that the same person performs the tasks of Dev and Ops. ** Developers ** and ** Operational ** are required to ** collaborate ** in order to ** benefit ** from ** ** skills ** and to improve ** empathy **.

HUMAN

[ PAIN ]

** heavy tasks ** are performed ** by the product team **.

Automation follows.

HUMAN

[ PAIN ]

In a traditional organization, ** lack of understanding ** between teams is usually related to distance and ** lack of communication **.

The ** members of a product team ** are ** co-responsible ** and ** solidary ** for all tasks.

** Pain ** is a key factor in ** Continuous Improvement **.

HUMAN

[ VALIDATION ]

The organization has a ** role of validation **, without being dogmatic.

HUMAN

[ VALIDATION ]

Ensure that the organization retains its ** validation role ** on tools and uses. In particular on the ** tools that affect the heritage ** (example: management of the source code).

** Provide ** Feature Teams with ** means ** to support their choices.

Do not be ** dogmatic ** and be sure to ** encourage experimentation **.

HUMAN

[ TRANSVERSALITY ]

Feature Teams are expected to ** communicate ** and share their ** experience ** and ** skills **.

HUMAN

[ TRANSVERSALITY ]

Do not create barriers between ** Feature Teams **.

Set up an ** organization ** and the ** agility ** needed for Feature Teams to communicate with each other and share their skills and experiences.

The organization of transversality at ** Spotify ** (Tribes, Chapters and Guilds) is an eloquent ** example.

INTEROPERABILITY

INTEROPERABILITY

INTEROPERABILITY

[ API FOR ALL ]

** APIs for all uses **: internal, customers and partners, public.

INTEROPERABILITY

[ API FOR ALL ]

Open your organization to new uses and new customers with ** Public APIs **.

In ** commercial ** partnerships **, ** customers ** as ** providers **, APIs are the standard exchange format.

** APIs ** are also intended to be used for ** internal uses ** of the organization.

INTEROPERABILITY

[ SELF SERVICE ]

Use an API must be ** simple ** and ** fast **.

INTEROPERABILITY

[ SELF SERVICE ]

The use of APIs should be as simple as possible. Think about the ** developer experience **.

The best solution to validate the adequacy with the need is to ** test the API quickly **: a few minutes must suffice!

The platform must offer a ** graphical interface ** to test the API simply.

INTEROPERABILITY

[ API MANAGEMENT ]

The uses of the APIs must be ** controlled ** and ** controlled **.

INTEROPERABILITY

[ API MANAGEMENT ]

Implement an API Management solution to manage ** quotas **, ** throttling **, ** authentication **, and ** logging **.

Collect metrics to manage ** monitoring **, ** filtering **, and ** reporting **.

INTEROPERABILITY

[ FEATURE FLIPPING ]

Create flexible and generic systems using ** feature flipping **.

INTEROPERABILITY

[ FEATURE FLIPPING ]

** feature flipping ** is about designing an app as a set of ** features ** that can be ** enabled ** or ** disabled ** hot, ** production **.

In a ** multi-tenant ** application, feature flipping allows you to ** customize ** supporters.

Feature flipping ** simplifies A / B testing **.

GOOD PRACTICES

GOOD PRACTICES

GOOD PRACTICES

[ TECHNICAL CHOICES ]

The ** technical choices ** are ** made ** and ** assumed ** by the ** Feature Team **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ TECHNICAL CHOICES ]

The Feature Team must act ** responsibly ** to identify the choices that impact it exclusively and the choices that impact the organization.

The ** choices ** that ** exceed the scope ** of the Feature Team (eg, license, infrequent programming language) must be ** validated ** by the organization or by the peer convergence process .

GOOD PRACTICES

[ GOOD USE ]

The ** right tool ** for ** good use ** is a source of savings.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ GOOD USE ]

A ** bad tool ** imposed on everyone is a ** risk **. The ** misuse ** of a good tool can have ** very ** damaging ** consequences **. For example, poorly used Agile methods are dangerous.

** tools ** must be ** questioned **.

** Excel ** is often a rational choice ** but it is not ** a tool to do everything ** (CRM, ERP, Datamart, …)

GOOD PRACTICES

[ OPEN SOURCE ]

** Make the most of Open Source **.

Alternative choices must be supported.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ OPEN SOURCE ]

The ** proprietary solutions ** are a ** risk ** for the organization that must be able to resume maintenance if needed.

There are few proprietary tools that do not have ** open source alternatives **.

The organization ** benefits ** from the ** Open Source Community ** and can ** pay back its contributions **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ MICRO-SERVICES ]

Develop ** stand-alone ** and ** weakly coupled ** services.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ MICRO-SERVICES ]

** weak coupling ** must be the norm.

Each micro-service has a ** clearly defined interface **.

This ** interface ** determines the ** link ** between the ** micro-services **.

** Domain Driven Design ** allows, especially with the ** Bounded Contexts **, to anticipate this problem.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ DATA ]

Each service has its own ** data storage system.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ DATA ]

A ** Data Store ** is intended to be ** coupled ** only with ** a single micro-service **.

The ** access to data ** from one micro-service to another is done ** exclusively via its interface **.

This design implies ** consistency over time ** across the platform. It must be ** apprehended at all levels **, including UX.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ PERIMETER ]

Each micro-service must have a reasonable functional perimeter, which ** "fits in the head" **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ PERIMETER ]

A micro-service offers a ** reasonable number of features **.

** Do not hesitate to cut ** a micro-service when it begins to grow.

A service of reasonable size makes it possible to ** consider ** serenely the ** rewriting **, if the need arises.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ EVENTS ]

The information system must be oriented ** events **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ EVENTS ]

The ** ** event-driven ** functional ** processes ** are ** naturally ** ** asynchronously ** implemented.

The ** event orientation ** allows to favor the implementation of approaches such as ** C ** ommand ** Q ** uery ** R ** esponsibility ** S ** egregation (** CQRS ** ) and ** Event Sourcing **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ CENTRALIZATION ]

The ** configuration ** of the services is ** centralized **, their ** discovery ** is ensured by a ** directory **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ CENTRALIZATION ]

The ** configuration ** of the ** micro-services ** is ** centralized ** for all ** environments **.

A central ** directory ** ensures ** dynamic discovery ** of ** micro-services **.

The ** ** global ** scalability ** depends on this ** directory **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ DESIGN FOR FAILURE ]

** Your system will crash! **

Design it so that it is tolerant.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ DESIGN FOR FAILURE ]

Your ** system will crash , it's inevitable. It must be designed for this ( Design For Failure **).

Predict ** redundancy ** at all levels: ** hardware ** (network, disk, etc.), ** applications ** (multiple instances of applications), ** ** geographies **, ** providers ** (example: AWS + OVH).

GOOD PRACTICES

[ TOOLKITS ]

Provide ** toolkits **, do not impose strict frameworks.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ TOOLKITS ]

** Attention to the technical components houses and transverse **! They are restrictive, expensive and difficult to maintain.

** accelerators **, ** toolkits **, ** technical stacks ** can be ** pooled **, ** free ** Feature Teams, avoiding a dogmatic approach.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ INFRASTRUCTURE ]

Feature Teams do not manage the infrastructure, it is ** provided and maintained by the organization **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ INFRASTRUCTURE ]

Infrastructure issues are not within ** Feature Teams **. Infrastructure must be ** provided ** and ** maintained ** by a ** cross-functional ** service.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ CONTAINERS ]

** Containers ** provide the flexibility needed for heterogeneous tooling.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ CONTAINERS ]

Containers provide the ** flexibility ** needed by Feature Teams to enable ** heterogeneous tooling ** in a ** homogeneous context **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ ENVIRONMENTS ]

The use of ** containers ** makes it possible to overcome the problems of ** technical environments **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ ENVIRONMENTS ]

The containers (example: ** Docker **) make it possible ** to be freed ** of the differences of environment.

The ** deployment ** process must be ** agnostic ** to the environment.

** Some components ** such as databases should not be deployed in containers. Their deployment is still automated.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ METRIC ]

Measures must be ** centralized ** and ** accessible ** to all.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ METRIC ]

The ** metrics ** are ** accessible ** to everyone with different levels of granularity: detailed view for the relevant Feature Team, aggregations for other members of the organization.

Access to ** metrics does not imply access to unit data **, it must be controlled to maintain confidentiality.

** All environments ** are affected.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ QUALITY ]

** software quality ** is a ** key factor **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ QUALITY ]

** code reviews ** are ** systematic **. They are conducted by members of the Feature Team or other members of the organization, as part of ** Continuous Improvement **.

That ** is not you being audited but your code **: "You are not your code!".

The ** qualimetry ** can be partly automated, but nothing beats the ** "new eye" **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ AUTOMATED TESTING ]

** automated testing ** is a non-negotiable prerequisite for continuous deployment.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ AUTOMATED TESTING ]

Automated ** testing ** ensures ** quality ** of the product ** over time **.

It is a ** prerequisite ** to continuous deployment, it allows ** ** changes ** and ** frequent deployments **.

The ** production rollout ** becomes an ** anecdotal ** event!

GOOD PRACTICES

[ TEST LEVELS ]

** Tests at all levels **: unit, integration, functional, resilience, performance.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ TEST LEVELS ]

The ** integration ** and ** functional ** tests are the most important, ** they ** guarantee ** the ** effective ** operation **.

** unit ** tests are suitable for ** development **.

** performance ** tests measure performance ** over time **.

** resilience ** tests help to anticipate ** failures **.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ BLANKET ]

** Cover ** is the primary objective indicator of test quality.

GOOD PRACTICES

[ BLANKET ]

The ** code coverage ** by the tests is a ** good ** metric of code quality.

This is a ** necessary condition ** but ** not sufficient **, the coverage of a ** bad ** test strategy can be high without guaranteeing the good quality of the code.
